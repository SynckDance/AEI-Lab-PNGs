This lab entry documents the development of Artificial Embodied Intelligence (AEI) within Global Movement Research—a series of frameworks, agents, and speculative systems designed to move the archive from storage to cognition.

The core question driving this work: what would it mean for a dance archive not only to preserve movement, but to interpret, compare, and respond alongside us?

The Agent Ecosystem

The Thinking Archive operates through six agents that together form what I call cognitive choreography. Each agent embodies a particular way of knowing—analytic, interpretive, archival, ethical, aesthetic, and reflective. Together, they form a distributed cognition system that processes motion, culture, and meaning as a continuum rather than separate domains.

The Analyzer reads movement data through QTC, SAM, and clustering methods. The Interpreter situates analyzed movement within cultural, historical, and geographic contexts. The Archivist manages storage, retrieval, and versioning. The Custodian serves as the ethical conscience, checking consent and cultural sensitivity. The Exhibitor curates and visualizes. The Companion works as a reflective research partner.

Intelligence is distributed and dialogic—the archive thinks only when these relations are active.

The Pipeline

The AEI pipeline moves through five stages: grounded data assemblage, structured encoding, AEI processing, recursive ethics review, and dissemination. A feedback loop ensures that community corrections, annotations, and withdrawals reshape how the system learns.

Motion capture, sonic recordings, oral histories, and geospatial layers enter the system and are encoded as QTC matrices, SAM sequences, pose vectors, and PPC metadata. These pass through the agent ecosystem before any output reaches the public. Every response is filtered through the Custodian.

Case Study: Ekombi

Ekombi, an Efik and Ibibio dance from Cross River and Akwa Ibom in Nigeria, serves as a test case for the Thinking Archive pipeline. The dance is characterized by curved, flowing articulation of torso and hips, with precise dialogue between arms, hands, and subtle weight shifts.

Ekombi enters the archive as situated practice—recorded through fieldwork, video, and motion capture alongside oral histories about its origins and ceremonial contexts. A QTC agent processes the recordings, converting observed trajectories into qualitative descriptions of movement relations: how the hips approach and recede, how the arms orbit the torso, how the body maintains or plays with proximity.

The SAM agent aligns these sequences across multiple recordings, identifying recurring patterns and meaningful variations. An ACF-based agent situates Ekombi within the Gulf of Guinea confluence, tracing coastal and inland routes of diffusion. The Interpreter synthesizes cultural context—when the dance functions as courtship, when it appears in festivals, how it encodes ideals of beauty and sociality among Efik and Ibibio people.

Ekombi's entry in the archive becomes a node in a network of movement patterns, confluence lines, comparative relations, and lived commentary—always open to community correction.

Activation Framework

The AEI Activation Framework organizes how archived movement can be activated beyond storage. It operates across four dimensions:

Reconstructive—using stored data to resurrect extinct or endangered dances, inferring missing transitions while respecting stylistic integrity. Diagnostic—treating movement as a window into physiological and psychological states, correlating qualities with patterns of fatigue, injury, or emotion. Cybernetic—using movement as biometric signature, where kinetic profiles can authenticate identity or serve as licensing marks. Speculative—exploring how dances might evolve in future ecologies where humans, machines, and environments co-create.

Each dimension carries distinct ethical stakes. All are governed by PPC ethics and Custodian review.

Process Artifacts

Materials in this lab include system diagrams, agent behavior notes, workflow pipelines connecting motion capture to AI inference, and draft interfaces for the Dance Confluence platform. Some are conceptual—asking what it would mean to simulate an extinct dance, model diffusion across generations, or treat movement signatures as identity protocols. Others are technical—documenting how QTC sequences, SAM outputs, and PPC metadata are structured so that an AEI system can work with them transparently and accountably.

This entry does not resolve these questions. It functions as a working notebook for the next stage of GMR, where the archive is treated as a living biome of movement and relation rather than a static container.
